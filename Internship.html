Work Integrated Learning Reflection

In the final year of my degree, I took place in the Work Integrated Learning industry placement project organised by Griffith University. The WIL industry placement in conducted each trimester as an opportunity for students to take place in an industry level placement under professional supervision with the expectations that students will take place in a substantial amount of work that develops the student's knowledge expected in a professional environment. This is a reflection on my experiences during the placement, not only including the new skills, knowledge and understanding of the formal workplace I discovered, but also many of the preconceptions I had prior to completing the WIL placement, that were challenged throughout.

During the first few weeks I spent at the placement I was exposed to an entire ecosystem of technology and software I was unfamiliar with. To be frank, this both appeared to be quite intimidating yet very exciting. Due to my unique circumstances, I was in the office four days a week compared to most students two days, therefore I used the extra time I was given to really focus on understanding how the different system interacted with each other. It was so much information to consume that our team has its own Wikipedia within the ATO's Microsoft Office SharePoint. Moreover, the work that I would be interacting with for the latter part of the placement and afterwards was through a virtual machine that used its own unique set of software and tools that I would have to learn. Despite the initial intimidation that I felt, I can honestly say that so far, I have enjoyed my time during the placement as it has given me the opportunity to work in an industry I enjoy while also giving me the freedom to learn and explore.

A major preconception that I had and struggled throughout the placement to fully comprehend was the idea that even industry professionals don't know all the answers. I cannot legitimately explain why I expected this to be the case however shortly after beginning the placement I started to question the other employees on how and why systems used within the placement functioned. Most of the time they were able to provide an answer, yet a few times the questions I had could not be answered when first asked. This initially did shock me; however, I slowly began to understand that my co-workers used these questions as both moments of reflection, and excuses to further develop their own knowledge. This thirst for continual improvement that my co-workers displayed helped in breaking down the preconception I had which in turn I believe helped me view my co-workers as people I could share these experiences with.

Another preconception I had was towards the level that industry standards were followed and adhered to. While I personally believe that my placement coordinated and functioned at a reasonably high level, there were still moments throughout the placement, during tasks where I had thought to myself, that surely, an organisation as large as the one I had placed with, one on a government level would function entirely off the industry standards. I wouldn't call the practices I observed ad-hoc, however they functioned outside my previous understanding and in most cases were uniquely situated to provide efficiency in greater quantities that standard procedures most likely would have achieved.

I would argue that these preconceptions I had towards the IT industry were shattered by my experiences during the placement. However, I refuse to think of these as negative realisations, but proof that a workplace can still provide value even if the means aren’t necessarily standard or conventional. With these newfound realisations I believe I will be able to practise mindfulness in my future experiences, I plan to be more understanding of others, knowing that while I shouldn’t expect them to know everything, I can still value new knowledge they are able to teach me. I also plan to show a greater willingness to accept the processes employed across the organisation as ones I can trust, but also understand the merit that there is most likely a valuable reason these processes are in place.

Upon reflection, I believe that the skills I have learnt and the preconceptions I have shattered have helped in preparing me for the transition from university to a graduate role. I was lucky enough to secure a one-year contract with the Australian Taxation Office, and I plan to use what I have learnt so far as a starting point in my career. I can only hope that I continue to learn and change in ways that make me a better, more valuable person.

Technical and Transferable Skills

Throughout the time I spent working for the Australian Taxation Office during the WIL placement I was able to develop many different technical and transferrable skills, some of these I had prior experience in and yet many others I have learnt throughout the time I spent during the placement, from my manager, my co-workers, and even myself by means of reflection.

A technical skill which I have further developed through the placement is the programming language SQL. For context, I spent almost a year in my degree studying SQL, I surprised myself with how much I enjoyed studying a back-end language and by the end of the year I could confidently say that I had a strong grasp on the language and knew a decent amount of it. However, that was in 2020, and with no reason to continue practising the language in my personal life I eventually forgot a majority of what made up SQL. In my defence I knew what I had forgotten was surface level knowledge, and that if at any time I were to practise I believe would quickly remember. However, this did not stop me from feeling overwhelmed and unaccomplished when it came time to use those skills throughout the placement. Compared to my colleagues, I was a child, their experience and knowledge far outweighed mine even at the peak of my learning and during the first few weeks I had doubts that I was not good enough to participate in the placement. These doubts, however, gave room to action. I decided that I would not let myself be consumed by the fear of inadequacy. I realised that I was surrounded by people that were the perfect teachers, from basic SQL down to the unique environments I would be using it for, my colleagues provided not only support, but a great platform for learning, that would also increase my knowledge of the work environment. Therefore, by accepting this placement opportunity I was able to learn, and further develop my knowledge of SQL, while also growing a greater bond with those I worked with.

A transferrable skill which I value as both a very important discipline and a great sign of respect within the workforce is time management. I believe that understanding one's own time management is not only a skill that benefits the individual but one that is greatly valued in a professional environment. For context, on Friday the 22nd of April our team was notified of an issue with the live production environment that had enabled users to commit fraud. Given the severity of the situation our team was placed under immense pressure to develop a fix for the issue in a timely manner, that in any other circumstances would have taken a month to develop and finalise. However, in utilising good time management, amongst other skills, our team was able to develop a fix within the week, accounting for a public holiday too. I believe this feat was only achievable due to the effort our team took in practicing good time management techniques, these included daily stand up / end of day meetings, constant updates to the schedule and reflection on what was achieved throughout the day, and how that reflection was implemented into the team's outlook calendar.

Overall, I believe this experience has further developed my understanding of what good time management is and how it is implemented. I believe time management can be defined as a transferrable skill due to all the situations where it is important, be it your work life, your university education and even your personal life. I understand more so from this experience that continuing to practice good time management will help me throughout my career by being able to keep on track with what is required of me in a professional environment. I also believe both time management and the knowledge of SQL are important skills required in the field of Networks and Security due to the fast-paced nature of the line of work, whether that be in securing services you work with, attempting to retrieve data that has been stolen, trying to catch those who have used the technology illegally and protecting databases with the appropriate SQL techniques. I believe by participating in the WIL placement I have been able to both learn new skills while further developing those I already had experience with and to me that shows I still have a lot to learn about both the industry and my future career goals.
